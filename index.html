Coalition for a National Fact-Checking Institute

The Problem: The quality of democratic governance in the United States is deteriorating. Like termites eating away at the foundations of a house, electronic amplification of lies and misinformation is stealthily eroding the foundations of American democracy. Congress must act now to address the problem before it is too late. The First Amendment imposes significant constraints, but Congress can enact legislation that is constitutionally valid and that could significantly reduce the harm caused by electronic amplification of misinformation related to elections and public health.

Disinformation is the New Nuclear Threat

In my early career, I worked for the federal government on nuclear proliferation and nuclear arms control issues because I feared that nuclear weapons posed a threat to humanity and I wanted to help mitigate that threat. For the past few years, I have devoted much of my time as a scholar to the problem of disinformation. Today, I fear that traditional tools of disinformation, combined with modern technologies for electronic amplification, pose a significant threat to democracy in the United States and elsewhere. I created the Coalition for a National Fact-Checking Institute to help mitigate that threat. I hope you agree that my proposed legislation would help reduce the threat to democracy presented by the electronic amplification of misinformation.

The Proposed Solution: Create an independent, non-governmental, non-profit organization called the National Fact-Checking Institute (NFCI), modelled on the National Endowment for Democracy. Like the Endowment, NFCI will not be an agency or instrumentality of the U.S. government. Also like the Endowment, though, NFCI’s activities will be regulated by a federal statute that provides significant congressional support for the organization. Whereas the Endowment helps promote democracy abroad, NFCI will help preserve the integrity of democratic governance at home by curbing the electronic amplification of misinformation related to elections and public health.

The statute, called the National Fact-Checking Institute Act, will regulate the private companies that provide electronic amplification services for individuals. Under the statute, those companies are known as “electronic megaphone companies,” or “EMCs.” EMCs include companies that provide electronic amplification services for messages transmitted via radio, television, social media, blogs, and podcasts.

NFCI’s operations will be entirely consistent with the First Amendment because NFCI will not exercise any coercive power to restrict speech. To the contrary, NFCI will engage in expressive activities that are constitutionally protected under the First Amendment. Specifically, NFCI will accomplish its mission by:

  • Monitoring the public communications of “persons with large electronic megaphones” (also known as “PLEMs,” a statutorily defined term)
  • Identifying public communications by PLEMs that present false or misleading claims related to elections and/or public health;
  • Issuing warnings to PLEMs and to the EMCs that provide electronic amplification services for them;
  • Encouraging, but not requiring, EMCs to reduce the electronic amplification of speech by PLEMs who repeatedly disseminate false or misleading claims after having received warnings; and
  • Providing detailed reports to Congress, the Federal Communications Commission (FCC), and the public at large.

What Would the Statute Accomplish? The statute does not impose civil or criminal penalties on PLEMs who persistently disseminate false or misleading claims, nor does it impose any penalties on EMCs who continue to provide electronic amplification services for such PLEMs. Hence, skeptics may wonder whether the statute would actually reduce the harm caused by electronic amplification of misinformation related to elections and public health. Empirical research shows that, at least in some cases, “naming and shaming” governments that violate internationally accepted norms has a positive effect on their behavior. Similarly, by “naming and shaming” PLEMs and EMCs in its reports to Congress, NFCI would likely induce some PLEMs to refrain from spreading lies and misinformation, and it would likely induce some EMCs to curb the electronic amplification of PLEMs who continue to disseminate false and misleading claims.

Moreover, the fact that the statute does not impose civil or criminal penalties on PLEMs or EMCs is a feature, not a bug. The absence of such penalties is a key feature that will help the statute survive constitutional scrutiny under the First Amendment. NFCI’s reports to Congress will provide a factual basis for Congress to determine—after the law has been implemented for a few years—whether a statutory scheme that relies primarily on naming and shaming provides a sufficiently strong remedy to protect American democracy. Congress might ultimately decide, based on NFCI’s reporting, that civil and/or criminal penalties are necessary to protect our democracy from the danger posed by electronic amplification of misinformation. In that case, an amended statute including such penalties would be much more likely to survive a First Amendment challenge because the government could argue that it tried less restrictive means (naming and shaming), but those less restrictive means did not achieve a significant reduction in the electronic amplification of lies and misinformation related to elections and public health.

Text of Proposed Legislation

This Act may be cited as the “National Fact-Checking Institute Act.”

The purpose of this Act is to protect American democracy from the electronic amplification of dangerous lies and misinformation related to elections and public health. Since it would pose unacceptable risks to free speech to empower any executive branch agency or official to decide on a case-by-case basis whether a particular claim is true or false, the Act delegates that responsibility to an independent, non-governmental, nonprofit corporation known as the National Fact-Checking Institute (NFCI).

Constitutional Analysis of Proposed Legislation

The attached paper analyzes three potential claims that could be raised to challenge the constitutional validity of the proposed National Fact-Checking Institute Act. The first one involves claims by “electronic megaphone companies” (EMCs). The next two involve claims by “persons with large electronic megaphones” (PLEMs). For the reasons explained in the attached paper, I conclude that the potential claims by PLEMs would almost certainly fail. The outcome of a First Amendment claim by EMCs is more difficult to predict, but the proposed statute, if enacted, would likely survive constitutional scrutiny.
